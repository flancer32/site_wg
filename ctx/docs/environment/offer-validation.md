## Purpose

Describe operational validation assumptions for narrow commercial offers before they become durable product or implementation commitments.

## Current Focus

The current focus is the `Agent Orchestration PoC` under `GitHub Flows`.

The first version is testing whether technically capable repository owners want to pay for a small bounded trial instead of building their own first AI-agent workflow environment immediately.

The offer is not testing generic excitement about AI agents.
It is testing whether a practical repository-scoped trial with GitHub-visible outputs, logs, and a short report is commercially meaningful.

## Validation Assumptions

The current validation assumes:

- buyers want a safe way to try AI agents on real GitHub issues;
- buyers care about seeing observable GitHub results and logs, not only a final comment;
- buyers are willing to pay `€50` for a bounded engineering check;
- direct repository-owner language should convert better than orchestration theory;
- some buyers will use the PoC to choose a later technical direction rather than to buy only the PoC itself.

## Success Signals

Useful validation signals include:

- meaningful repository submissions rather than generic curiosity;
- visitors who clearly describe what they want to test;
- at least one paid PoC;
- visitors who show understanding of `1` repository and up to `5` issues before submitting;
- buyers who ask follow-up questions about self-hosted, local-model, GitLab, prompt, label, or workflow customization;
- requests that connect the PoC to a real issue workflow rather than vague AI interest.

## Failure Signals

Important validation failure signals include:

- page interest without request intent;
- requests that show confusion about what the offer actually is;
- repeated fear around repository access that the page does not resolve;
- requests that expect code changes, pull requests, or production deployment inside the `€50` scope;
- requests that treat the form as a place to negotiate a large custom workflow before first review;
- paid PoCs that generate no useful continuation or learning.

## Review Questions

Early requests should help answer:

- which issue scenarios buyers actually want to try first;
- whether visible logs materially increase trust and understanding;
- whether the `€50` framing is clear enough;
- whether the safety message is visible early enough to reduce repository-access hesitation;
- whether public/test repository preference blocks or helps conversion;
- which follow-up direction appears most often after first buyer interest.

## Expected Adjustment Areas

After first visitors or leads, likely adjustment areas include:

- first-screen wording;
- CTA wording around the `€50` PoC request;
- the degree of emphasis on logs versus final results;
- explanation of safety boundaries;
- clarity of included and excluded scope;
- form length and friction;
- explanation of likely next technical directions after the trial.

The first version should stay small.
It should not expand into a larger product page until real request patterns justify that expansion.
